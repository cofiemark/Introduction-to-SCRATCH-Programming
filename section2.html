Scratch Interface Overview

The Scratch interface is designed to be intuitive and user-friendly, making it accessible for beginners of all ages.

The main components of the Scratch interface

The Scratch interface consists of several key areas:

  • Stage: The area where your projects run, with dimensions of 480 × 360 pixels. The stage has its own x-y coordinate system with (0,0) at the center.
  • Block Palette: Contains all the programming blocks, organized by category and color-coded for easy identification.
  • Scripts Area: The workspace where you drag and connect blocks to create scripts (programs).
  • Sprites Pane: Shows all the characters and objects in your project. Each sprite can have its own scripts, costumes, and sounds.
  • Tabs: Switch between Scripts, Costumes/Backdrops, and Sounds for the selected sprite or stage.

Online vs. Offline Editor

Scratch is available in two formats: an online editor accessed through a web browser and an offline editor that can be downloaded and installed on your computer.

Online Editor

  • Access through any web browser at scratch.mit.edu
  • Automatically saves projects to your Scratch account
  • Easy sharing and access to the Scratch community
  • Regular updates with new features
  • Requires internet connection

Offline Editor

  • Downloadable application for Windows, macOS, and Linux
  • Works without internet connection
  • Projects saved locally on your computer
  • Useful for classrooms with limited internet access
  • May not have the latest features immediately

Both versions offer the same core functionality, so the choice depends on your specific needs and constraints.

Creating a Scratch Account

To fully utilize Scratch, especially the online version, it's recommended to create a Scratch account.

Steps to Create an Account:

  1. Visit scratch.mit.edu and click "Join Scratch" in the top menu
  2. Create a username and password
  3. Provide basic information as requested
  4. Verify your account through email (for users over 13) or parent/guardian email (for users under 13)

Benefits of Having an Account:

  • Save projects to the cloud
  • Share projects with the Scratch community
  • Comment on and remix others' projects
  • Join Scratch studios (collections of projects)
  • Follow other Scratch users

For classroom settings, educators can create teacher accounts that provide additional features for managing student accounts and projects.

Navigating the Scratch Environment

Top Menu Bar

The top menu bar provides navigation to different sections of the Scratch website:

  • Scratch Logo: Returns to the Scratch homepage
  • Create: Opens the Scratch editor
  • Explore: Browse projects shared by the community
  • Ideas: Tutorials and project ideas
  • About: Information about Scratch
  • Search: Find projects and users
  • User Menu: Access account options, your projects, and studios

Editor Menu Bar

Within the Scratch editor, the menu bar includes:

  • File: Save, load, and download projects
  • Edit: Undo, redo, and other editing functions
  • Tutorials: Access guided tutorials
  • Language Selector: Change the interface language

Stage, Sprites, and Backdrops

Stage

The Stage is the area where your Scratch projects come to life. It has several important characteristics:

  • Dimensions: 480 pixels wide by 360 pixels tall
  • Coordinate system: (0,0) at the center, with x ranging from -240 to 240 (left to right) and y ranging from -180 to 180 (bottom to top)
  • Can have its own scripts, backdrops, and sounds
  • Cannot move like sprites can

Sprites

Sprites are the characters or objects that perform actions in your projects:

  • Each sprite has its own scripts, costumes, and sounds
  • Properties include position, direction, size, and visibility
  • Can interact with other sprites and the stage
  • Can be created from the library, drawn, uploaded, or captured from a camera

Backdrops

Backdrops are the background images for the stage:

  • Multiple backdrops allow for scene changes
  • Can be selected from the library, drawn, uploaded, or captured
  • Switching backdrops can trigger events in your project
